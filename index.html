The shot that changed how the game was played

The three-point line was introduced in the NBA in 1979, but for decades it remained a secondary weapon rather than a foundational offensive strategy. Through the 1990s and early 2000s, the mid-range jumper was the defining shot of the era — players like Kobe Bryant, Michael Jordan, and Dirk Nowitzki built their legacies on pull-up jumpers from 15 to 20 feet.

Teams were averaging just 13 to 14 three-point attempts per game at the turn of the millennium, and the shot was largely reserved for designated shooters or end-of-shot-clock bailouts. The offensive philosophy of that era prioritized post play, isolation scoring, and the mid-range game, which coaches viewed as high-percentage, controllable offense

The analytical revolution — and one player in particular — changed everything. The rise of advanced metrics in the early 2010s exposed a brutal truth about the mid-range jumper: it is statistically the least efficient shot in basketball. A mid-range attempt yields roughly 0.8 points per possession, while a three-pointer, even made at a modest 35% clip, generates 1.05 points per possession.

Then came Stephen Curry. More than any spreadsheet or front office mandate, Curry made the three-point shot culturally irresistible. His ability to shoot off the dribble from logo range, at high volume and at an absurd percentage — peaking at 45.4% on 11 attempts per game in his record-breaking 2015-16 season — forced every team in the league to reconsider how they built rosters and designed offenses.

The ripple effect has been nothing short of a complete transformation of professional basketball. In 2024-25, the Boston Celtics averaged 48 three-point attempts per game, by far the most in the league and the most ever recorded in a single season. League-wide, teams went from 13.7 attempts per game in 2000 to roughly 35 per game today — a 155% increase in volume with shooting percentage holding steady, proving the analytics right. The mid-range jumper has not disappeared entirely, but it has been demoted from centerpiece to situational tool. What Curry demonstrated, analytics confirmed, and the rest of the league followed: in today's NBA, the three-point shot is no longer a specialty — it is the foundation of how the game is played.
